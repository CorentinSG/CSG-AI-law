# Federal New York AI Litigation Developments: Privilege, Work Product, Confidentiality, Discovery, Protective Orders, Sanctions, and GenAI Use

Research date: July 19, 2026
Agent: NY-FEDERAL-WORK-PRODUCT
Scope: Second Circuit, S.D.N.Y., E.D.N.Y., N.D.N.Y., W.D.N.Y., and closely related 2025-2026 discovery leads where secondary alerts specifically identify privilege/work-product/protective-order issues.

## Executive Signal

Federal New York law is developing along two tracks. First, the Second Circuit and New York federal district courts are now highly clear that AI-generated fake authority triggers Rule 11 and candor duties; the practical question is usually sanction severity, not whether counsel had a duty to verify. Second, privilege/work-product questions are less settled. The most important verified federal New York authority is United States v. Heppner, where S.D.N.Y. held that a criminal defendant's self-directed exchanges with a public GenAI platform were not attorney-client privileged or work product. Secondary 2026 alerts contrast Heppner with Warner v. Gilbarco and Morgan v. V2X, but Warner and Morgan are non-New York district court decisions and should be treated as persuasive comparators, not federal New York authority.

## Verified Federal New York Authorities

### 1. United States v. Heppner, No. 1:25-cr-00503-JSR, 2026 WL 436479 (S.D.N.Y. Feb. 17, 2026)

- Court/date: S.D.N.Y., Judge Jed S. Rakoff, February 17, 2026.
- Source URL: CourtListener docket, https://www.courtlistener.com/docket/71872024/united-states-v-heppner/ ; RECAP PDF, https://storage.courtlistener.com/recap/gov.uscourts.nysd.652138/gov.uscourts.nysd.652138.27.0.pdf
- Primary-source confidence: High for existence/outcome; medium for granular reasoning because the public PDF was located but not machine-text extracted in this run.
- Legal issue: Whether defendant-generated exchanges with a GenAI platform were protected by attorney-client privilege or work-product doctrine.
- Holding/signal: The docket text states the Court granted the Government's motion that certain written exchanges with a GenAI platform were not protected from Government inspection by either attorney-client privilege or work product. The key limiting facts reported in public commentary are that the defendant used the AI tool on his own volition, not as counsel's agent or at counsel's direction.
- Practical signal: Public GenAI prompts/outputs are a privilege hazard when a client or witness uses them independently. Counsel should give written client instructions forbidding unsupervised use of public GenAI for legal strategy, defense narratives, witness preparation, or privileged fact summaries.
- Suggested tags/legal areas: attorney-client privilege; work product; criminal discovery; GenAI prompts; client AI use; public AI tools; waiver risk.

### 2. Park v. Kim, 91 F.4th 610 (2d Cir. Jan. 30, 2024)

- Court/date: Second Circuit, January 30, 2024.
- Source URL: Justia, https://law.justia.com/cases/federal/appellate-courts/ca2/22-2057/22-2057-2024-01-30.html ; CourtListener cited by aggregator, https://www.courtlistener.com/opinion/9469987/park-v-kim/
- Primary-source confidence: High.
- Legal issue: Appellate counsel cited a nonexistent case generated by ChatGPT; court addressed Rule 11 and candor obligations.
- Holding/signal: The Second Circuit referred counsel to its Grievance Panel and directed counsel to provide the decision to her client. The court stated that Rule 11 requires attorneys to read and confirm the existence and validity of authorities they rely on.
- Practical signal: Park is the controlling Second Circuit anchor for citation-verification obligations. It makes local AI disclosure rules unnecessary to establish the baseline duty.
- Suggested tags/legal areas: Second Circuit; Rule 11; appellate practice; hallucinated citations; attorney discipline; client notice.

### 3. Mata v. Avianca, Inc., 678 F. Supp. 3d 443 (S.D.N.Y. June 22, 2023)

- Court/date: S.D.N.Y., Judge P. Kevin Castel, June 22, 2023.
- Source URL: Justia, https://law.justia.com/cases/federal/district-courts/new-york/nysdce/1%3A2022cv01461/575368/54/
- Primary-source confidence: High.
- Legal issue: Attorneys filed submissions relying on fake cases and fake quotations generated through ChatGPT.
- Holding/signal: Rule 11 sanctions imposed; the court found subjective bad faith, including conscious avoidance, misleading statements, and continued reliance after warnings. Sanctions included a $5,000 penalty and notice/service obligations.
- Practical signal: Mata remains the canonical federal New York sanctions case. It treats reliance on fake opinions as an abuse of the adversary system, particularly where counsel does not promptly correct the problem.
- Suggested tags/legal areas: Rule 11; inherent powers; sanctions; ChatGPT; hallucinated citations; subjective bad faith; duty to correct.

### 4. United States v. Cohen, 724 F. Supp. 3d 251, 2024 WL 1193604 (S.D.N.Y. Mar. 20, 2024)

- Court/date: S.D.N.Y., Judge Jesse M. Furman, March 20, 2024.
- Source URL: Official S.D.N.Y. PDF identified by Legal AI Governance, https://www.nysd.uscourts.gov/sites/default/files/2024-03/18cr602%20Cohen%20Opinion.pdf ; lead page, https://legalaigovernance.com/tracker/cases/us-v-cohen-sdny/
- Primary-source confidence: Medium-high; official PDF identified, secondary page used for concise retrieval metadata.
- Legal issue: Sentencing/post-conviction filing included fake citations supplied by Michael Cohen after using Google Bard.
- Holding/signal: Sanctions declined because the court did not find subjective bad faith by counsel under Second Circuit standards, distinguishing Mata-like concealment or conscious avoidance.
- Practical signal: Cohen is the main federal New York boundary case: negligent failure to catch AI-generated citations can avoid sua sponte Rule 11 sanctions where counsel is candid, reasonably misunderstood the source, and accepts responsibility. It is not a safe harbor for current practice.
- Suggested tags/legal areas: criminal practice; Rule 11; no sanctions; Google Bard; client-supplied research; subjective bad faith.

### 5. Benjamin v. Costco Wholesale Corp., 779 F. Supp. 3d 341, 2025 WL 1195925 (E.D.N.Y. Apr. 24, 2025)

- Court/date: E.D.N.Y., Magistrate Judge Lee G. Dunst, April 24, 2025.
- Source URL: FindLaw free opinion, https://caselaw.findlaw.com/court/us-dis-crt-ed-new-yor/117206693.html ; CourtListener docket cited by aggregator, https://www.courtlistener.com/docket/69302907/benjamin-v-costco-wholesale-corporation/
- Primary-source confidence: High.
- Legal issue: Plaintiff's counsel used ChatOn to draft a remand reply and filed fake cases in a sworn affirmation without reading or cite-checking them.
- Holding/signal: Court found subjective bad faith and imposed $1,000 payable to the court, plus client service of the order. The opinion stresses that attorneys cannot delegate core attorney-client duties to AI and file the output without review.
- Practical signal: Candid apology and CLE mitigate sanction severity, but they do not erase Rule 11 exposure. Sworn AI-generated filings worsen risk.
- Suggested tags/legal areas: E.D.N.Y.; Rule 11; ChatOn; client notice; sanctions; paralegal supervision; sworn filing.

### 6. Ramirez v. Humala, No. 24-cv-242-RPK-JAM, 2025 WL 1384161 (E.D.N.Y. May 13, 2025)

- Court/date: E.D.N.Y., Judge Rachel P. Kovner, May 13, 2025.
- Source URL: RECAP PDF identified in public tracker, https://storage.courtlistener.com/recap/gov.uscourts.nyed.508684/gov.uscourts.nyed.508684.49.0.pdf ; tracker lead, https://legalaigovernance.com/tracker/cases/ramirez-v-humala/
- Primary-source confidence: Medium-high; public RECAP PDF located, but full text not extracted in this run.

- Suggested tags/legal areas: E.D.N.Y.; Rule 11; nonlawyer supervision; paralegal AI use; hallucinated citations; sanctions.

### 7. Kaur v. Desso, No. 9:25-cv-00726-AMN, 2025 WL 1895859 (N.D.N.Y. July 9, 2025)

- Court/date: N.D.N.Y., Judge Anne M. Nardacci, July 9, 2025.
- Source URL: Justia docket/order, https://docs.justia.com/cases/federal/district-courts/new-york/nyndce/9%3A2025cv00726/148505/24 ; GovInfo PDF, https://www.govinfo.gov/content/pkg/USCOURTS-nynd-9_25-cv-00726/pdf/USCOURTS-nynd-9_25-cv-00726-0.pdf
- Primary-source confidence: High.
- Legal issue: Immigration habeas counsel used Claude Sonnet 4 to draft supplemental briefing containing fabricated quotations from real authorities.
- Holding/signal: Court found Rule 11 violation and subjective bad faith, imposed $1,000, required AI/legal-writing CLE, and required service of the order on the client. The court rejected deadline pressure and illness as excuses for not verifying AI output.
- Practical signal: Fabricated quotations from real cases are treated like fabricated cases. Emergency litigation does not excuse verification failures.
- Suggested tags/legal areas: N.D.N.Y.; Rule 11; Claude; immigration habeas; fabricated quotations; emergency briefing; client notice; CLE.

### 8. Flycatcher Corp. Ltd. v. Affable Avenue LLC, No. 24-cv-9429-KPF, 2026 U.S. Dist. LEXIS 23980 (S.D.N.Y. Feb. 5, 2026)

- Court/date: S.D.N.Y., Judge Katherine Polk Failla, February 5, 2026.
- Source URL: Justia, https://law.justia.com/cases/federal/district-courts/new-york/nysdce/1%3A2024cv09429/633195/227/
- Primary-source confidence: High.
- Legal issue: Counsel repeatedly filed briefs and responses containing false citations and false quotation/citation attributions after warnings and an order to show cause.
- Holding/signal: Court imposed terminal sanctions under Rule 11 and inherent powers by striking Affable's submissions and entering default judgment against the client; attorneys' fees against counsel were also invited. The court credited AI hallucination as the only coherent explanation and found bad faith.
- Practical signal: Repeated AI-citation failures after warnings can become case-ending misconduct. Courts may bind the client to counsel's AI misuse.
- Suggested tags/legal areas: S.D.N.Y.; terminal sanctions; default judgment; Rule 11; inherent powers; AI cite-checking; vLex; Paxton AI; counsel misconduct.

### 9. Jimenez-Fogarty v. Fogarty, No. 24-cv-8705-JLR-GWG (S.D.N.Y. Apr. 29, 2026)

- Court/date: S.D.N.Y., Magistrate Judge Gabriel W. Gorenstein, April 29, 2026.
- Source URL: Justia, https://law.justia.com/cases/federal/district-courts/new-york/nysdce/1%3A2024cv08705/631979/192/
- Primary-source confidence: High.
- Legal issue: Attorney filed memoranda with numerous fabricated citations, gave no coherent explanation of how they were created, and later filed additional papers with false citations.
- Holding/signal: Rule 11 sanctions of $2,500; client service; notice to judges in other pending cases; sworn compliance statement. The opinion infers AI use because the only plausible sources of fabricated citations were deliberate human fabrication or an AI system.
- Practical signal: Failure to identify the AI/tooling or drafting process can itself aggravate sanctions. Courts may require notice in other cases to protect other dockets.
- Suggested tags/legal areas: S.D.N.Y.; Rule 11; fabricated citations; inferred AI use; notice to other courts; attorney candor; bad faith.

## Non-New York Comparators Specifically Mentioned In 2025-2026 Alerts

### 14. Warner v. Gilbarco, Inc., No. 2:24-cv-12333, 2026 WL 373043 (E.D. Mich. Feb. 10, 2026)

- Court/date: E.D. Mich., Magistrate Judge Anthony P. Patti, February 10, 2026.
- Source URL: Justia, https://law.justia.com/cases/federal/district-courts/michigan/miedce/2%3A2024cv12333/379552/94/
- Primary-source confidence: High, but non-New York.
- Legal issue: Whether a pro se litigant's AI-assisted litigation materials were discoverable and whether use of ChatGPT/public AI waived work product; sanctions/protective-order dispute over alleged confidential uploads.
- Holding/signal: Court denied sanctions; public snippets state defendants had no evidence plaintiff uploaded confidential documents to AI. Secondary alerts report the court treated AI-assisted materials as work product and rejected waiver absent disclosure to an adversary or likely adversary access.
- Practical signal: Warner is the main comparator that cuts the other way from Heppner on work-product waiver. It is persuasive only in federal New York and distinguishable because it involved a civil pro se litigant's litigation preparation rather than a criminal defendant's self-directed AI exchanges.
- Suggested tags/legal areas: work product; ChatGPT; protective order; pro se; sanctions denied; non-New York comparator.

## Practical Takeaways For Federal New York Litigation

1. Treat AI prompt/output records as potentially discoverable ESI. Heppner and NYT/OpenAI show that courts will not assume confidentiality merely because the material is conversational or deleted by user expectation.
2. Add GenAI instructions to engagement letters, litigation holds, and witness-prep protocols. Clients should not feed legal strategy, privileged facts, draft declarations, or confidential discovery into public GenAI tools absent counsel-approved safeguards.
3. Protective orders should address AI expressly. Minimum language should prohibit uploading confidential/protected material to public or training-enabled GenAI systems and should permit only secure tools with no training, retention, or third-party access inconsistent with the order.
4. Work-product protection turns on purpose, direction, and disclosure risk. AI used by counsel or a party as a tool for litigation preparation is more defensible than unsupervised, self-directed AI conversations with a public system.
5. Citation verification is now table stakes in the Second Circuit. Park, Mata, Benjamin, Kaur, Flycatcher, and Jimenez-Fogarty make clear that every cited case and quoted proposition must be independently checked against primary law.
6. Remediation affects sanctions. Immediate candor, withdrawal/correction, CLE, client notice, and a concrete verification protocol can mitigate; delay, evasive explanations, repeat filings, and AI-generated OSC responses can trigger severe or terminal sanctions.
